★ Signature Project EPID 630 · Final Proposal · Dec 2024

Technology-Enabled Mobile Diabetes Self-Management Platform

Fundamentals of Population Health Informatics · Full proposal + presentation

The problem this solves
"Mississippi has a 13.6% diabetes prevalence rate vs. 10.5% nationally — and existing digital self-management tools weren't built for the population bearing the greatest burden."
Black American adults ages 35–55 in Jackson, MS face compounding barriers: rural healthcare deserts, food insecurity, limited access to culturally competent diabetes education, and technology tools designed for someone else.

This proposal designs a mobile-first platform that integrates Bluetooth-enabled glucose monitors (Dexcom), fitness wearables, and EHR data via HL7 FHIR APIs and SCRIPT pharmacy standards — then translates all of it into a "Spotify Wrapped"-style visual language that prioritizes health literacy over clinical jargon. The platform collects six data categories (demographic, clinical, medication, lifestyle, SDOH, behavioral) and routes them to both individual dashboards and an administrative population health layer for stakeholders and public health authorities.

A unified privacy governance framework exceeds HIPAA minimums, incorporates a community data ethics board with significant target-population representation, blockchain-based data sovereignty, and differential privacy for population-level analytics. Implementation follows a phased community co-design model — the Community Advisory Board shapes the interface before a line of code is written.

Reflection
The most important decision in this project wasn't technical — it was choosing not to replicate what already exists. Most diabetes apps are built for someone who already has resources, health literacy, and a supportive care team. This proposal required sitting with the question of why those tools fail this population specifically, and letting that answer drive the architecture. The "Spotify Wrapped" framing wasn't aesthetic — it was the answer to a real usability problem about how to present A1c trends to someone who has never seen a glucose chart before.
ArchitectureSix-category data model spanning demographic through behavioral variables, with semantic/syntactic standards for cross-source translation and HL7 FHIR for EHR interoperability
PrivacyLayered governance framework — HIPAA-grade encryption, differential privacy for population analytics, blockchain data sovereignty, and a community ethics board with veto power over new data uses
EquityCommunity co-design from day one — interface terminology, educational nudges, and goal-setting features all shaped by the target population before development begins
Group Project · Lead: App Architecture EPID 631 · Midterm + Final · 2025

EmpowerHer Health — AI-Enabled Maternal Health Companion App

Principles of Consumer Health Informatics

Key statistic driving this project
Black women face a maternal mortality rate of 55.3 per 100,000 live births — nearly 3× the rate for white women — and that number has continued to rise.
In NYC, Black women were 6× more likely to die of a pregnancy-related cause than white women from 2016–2020. The disparity isn't biological — it's structural, driven by implicit bias, medical mistrust, and a healthcare system that wasn't designed with them in mind.

Co-designed an AI-enabled progressive web application for Empower Her Health, a NYC-based org. Joseph led the innovation architecture and outcomes sections. The app is built on Google's Flutter for cross-platform parity, uses multi-factor authentication designed for Post-Roe data sensitivity, and features an encounter review tool that lets users describe a clinical visit and receive AI-based sentiment analysis flagging potential bias or red flags — turning a subjective, often dismissed experience into documented evidence.

Reflection
The encounter review feature was the most conceptually interesting design challenge in this project. The question was: how do you build a tool that validates lived experiences of bias without making causal claims a non-medical AI shouldn't make? The answer was sentiment analysis framed as pattern recognition — surfacing language associated with dismissiveness or stereotype-driven care, presented as "things to follow up with your provider" rather than diagnoses of bias.

Solo Design · 3 Wireframe Iterations EPID 633 · April 2025

Predictive Anxiety Symptom Tracker — mHealth PWA

mHealth & Consumer Informatics

Independently designed and iterated a low-fidelity prototype in Balsamiq targeting a specific gap: existing anxiety apps either oversimplify (step counters, breathing exercises) or overwhelm (full CBT therapy modules) — neither serves someone in the middle of an anxious episode who needs under-30-second logging and no forced therapeutic path. UCD process began with consulting individuals with lived anxiety experience.

Three documented generations: v1 (circular nav, sparse) → v2 (labeled buttons, added Settings) → v3 (adds "Today's Insight" predictive alert card surfacing pattern-based risk flags like "Monday mornings" and "Evenings after 8"). The View Trends screen combines mood-over-time line chart, locations bar chart, coping strategy pie, and ML-style high-risk period identification.

Reflection
The jump from v2 to v3 came from a realization mid-process: I was building a reactive tool in a space that needs proactive support. Logging anxiety after it happens is useful; knowing it's likely to happen Wednesday evening is more useful. That single insight — that the platform should use its own data to warn users before high-risk periods rather than just document what already occurred — changed the whole value proposition of the app.
UCD ProcessUser interviews before wireframing; iterative gaps identified through self-evaluation against user needs; final design emerged from, not before, the research
TechnicalProgressive Web App chosen over native: lower friction adoption, browser-based access, no app store dependency — critical for a mental health tool where stigma may prevent downloading a labeled "anxiety app"

Original Research · R · N=3,800 PUBH 614 · April 2025

Neighborhood Walkability, Mental Distress & Alcohol Use — NYC

Quantitative & Qualitative Data Analysis Methods in Public Health Research

Logistic regression analysis using the NYC Physical Activity and Transit (PAT) Survey (N=3,800 after cleaning). Full pipeline in R: data recoding via tidyverse and janitor, cross-tabulations, chi-squared tests, unadjusted and adjusted odds ratios with 95% CI via gtsummary, visualizations in ggplot2. Covariates (age, gender, income) included to test strength of association after adjustment.

Key finding
Low-walkability residents had 1.50× higher odds of frequent mental distress — and that held after adjusting for age, gender, and income.
The alcohol finding was the surprise: low-walkability residents had 26% lower odds of alcohol use. The likely explanation — reduced social connectivity in car-dependent environments — suggests walkability shapes health through isolation as much as physical activity.
# Adjusted logistic regression data %>% select(walkability, mental_distress, alcohol_use) %>% tbl_summary(by=walkability, missing="no") %>% add_p()
Fig. 1 — Alcohol use by neighborhood walkability. High-walkability neighborhoods show ~34% alcohol use vs. ~26% in low-walkability areas — counterintuitive until social connectivity is considered. (ggplot2, R)
Reflection
The alcohol finding forced a more honest interpretation than the mental distress finding did. Mental distress and low walkability aligning is intuitive — isolation, less movement, less access to green space. But the inverse alcohol relationship made me reconsider what walkability actually measures: not just pedestrian infrastructure, but the social texture of a neighborhood. High-walkability areas are denser, more social, more bar-accessible. That's a confounder I'd want to disentangle in a follow-up study with more granular neighborhood data.
MethodsUnadjusted and adjusted logistic regression; Pearson chi-squared; odds ratios with 95% CI; covariate selection grounded in DAG-style confounding rationale, not stepwise
ToolsR: tidyverse · janitor · gtsummary · ggplot2 · scales; reproducible pipeline from raw survey data to publication-ready tables and figures
Quarto Dashboard · Interactive Plotly EPID 634 · Final Project · 2025

Semaglutide Adoption vs. Obesity Burden — State-Level Dashboard

EPID 634 · Health Data Visualization

The policy-relevant finding
States with the highest obesity burden are not the states receiving the most GLP-1 prescriptions. High-prescribing states tend to cluster in lower-obesity quartiles.
Arkansas, Mississippi, and West Virginia — among the highest-obesity states — show prescribing volumes far below states like California and New York. This isn't a clinical finding, but it raises a direct access equity question for a drug class being positioned as a population-level obesity intervention.

Built a three-chart interactive Quarto dashboard comparing state-level GLP-1 prescribing (Medicare SDUD 2024, filtered for Ozempic/Wegovy/Rybelsus using stringr pattern matching) against BRFSS 2024 adult obesity prevalence. States joined by abbreviation after state-lookup table construction. Charts: scatter plot (prescriptions vs. obesity, quartile-colored), ranked bar chart by state, and a diverging rank-mismatch chart showing prescribing/burden alignment gaps.

Fig. 3a — Semaglutide prescriptions (log scale) vs. adult obesity prevalence by state, color-coded by obesity quartile. Green (lowest obesity) states like California and New York dominate the upper prescribing range.
Fig. 3b — Prescriptions by state ranked, color-coded by obesity group. Pink (highest obesity) states are largely absent from top prescribing ranks.
Fig. 3c — Rank mismatch between obesity burden and prescribing volume. Arkansas leads the gap — highest obesity rank, far lower prescribing rank.
Reflection
The hardest methodological choice here was resisting the urge to frame this as a causal story. The data shows a correlation between low-obesity states and high GLP-1 prescribing — but Medicare SDUD captures only Medicare-enrolled patients, which skews older and may not reflect the full prescribing picture. The honest frame is descriptive: here is a pattern that raises an access question worth investigating further, not a conclusion that high-obesity states are being underserved by prescribers.
ToolsQuarto dashboard format with plotly interactive charts; data wrangling via stringr regex filtering on product names, janitor for column standardization, manual state abbreviation lookup table
DataMedicare SDUD 2024 (CMS state utilization data) + BRFSS 2024 (CDC adult obesity prevalence by state); joined on state name after abbreviation-to-full-name conversion

ArcGIS · Five-Layer Spatial Analysis EPID 632 · Midterm · Dec 2025

Pedestrian Risk & Transportation Equity in Fulton County, GA

Health GIS & Spatial Analysis

Central finding
Crash hotspots are concentrated precisely where low-income residents walk to schools and grocery stores — close proximity to essential services is negated when the route there is dangerous.
In Central and South Fulton — Atlanta's most transit-dependent, lowest-income corridors — pedestrian and bicycle crashes cluster around the same locations as grocery stores and public schools. In North Fulton, wealthier and more car-dependent, crash activity is near zero. This isn't a coincidence; it's the spatial signature of infrastructure disinvestment.
Fig. 2 — Fulton County, GA: Pedestrian & bicycle crash heat map (yellow/red = highest density) overlaid with census tract income choropleth and essential destination points. The crash concentration in Central/South Fulton aligns directly with the highest walking/transit mode share and lowest median income tracts.  ·  View live interactive map →

Five data layers integrated: 2022 Propel ATL crash data, ACS 2022 transportation mode shares and median income by census tract, USDOE public school locations, ArcGIS-compiled grocery store listings — all joined to TIGER/Line census tract boundaries. The analysis was directed at the Fulton County Board of Health and City of Atlanta Urban Planning Office.

Reflection
The most instructive part of this analysis was discovering that it told a commonsense story the data had never been assembled to tell before. Everyone who lives in South Fulton knows it's dangerous to walk. The map's value isn't the finding — it's the evidence. A community organizer can take this to a county commissioner and point to a specific census tract where children walk past a crash hotspot to get to school. That's the difference between a claim and an argument.
GIS SkillsMulti-source data integration, spatial joins to TIGER/Line tract boundaries, heat map layer, choropleth by income, point layers for essential destinations — all combined into a single navigable interactive web map
Policy ImpactActionable recommendations delivered: protected bike lanes, pedestrian refuge islands, crosswalk additions, improved lighting, and expanded transit stops targeted to highest-risk South Fulton corridors

DISCERN · EPID 631

Consumer Health Information Quality — Web Audit

Principles of Consumer Health Informatics · March 2025

Systematic quality audit of 54 web pages across three search terms using Google Search (90% global market share), applying the DISCERN instrument across 16 criteria — reliability, treatment information quality, and shared decision-making support — to five evaluated sites: Cleveland Clinic, Johns Hopkins, NHS, Healthline, and Urology Care Foundation.

Key finding
No evaluated site scored 5/5 simultaneously on source transparency and uncertainty acknowledgment — the two criteria most predictive of health information trustworthiness.
The Urology Care Foundation ranked highest overall; .org domains dominated results (n=25 of 54 pages). Sites with the clearest aims and most comprehensive treatment coverage consistently failed to cite their evidence sources — a structural gap in how consumer health content is produced, not just presented.
Reflection
The most practically useful takeaway from this evaluation was about what high-visibility doesn't guarantee. Johns Hopkins and Cleveland Clinic — names that signal authority — scored below Healthline on several DISCERN criteria because their patient-facing content is often thinner than their clinical reputation implies. Brand trust and information quality are not the same thing, and that distinction matters enormously for building knowledge bases in tools like the EmpowerHer app.
FrameworkDISCERN: 16-item validated instrument for evaluating consumer health information quality across reliability, treatment completeness, and decision support — applied systematically across 54 pages

Mixed Methods Analysis · PUBH 614

Mixed Methods Analysis — Women's Mobility & Reproductive Health, Pakistan

Quantitative & Qualitative Data Analysis Methods · May 2025

Analyzed a published mixed methods study combining STATA bivariate analysis of the Pakistan Fertility and Family Planning Survey with five-month ethnographic fieldwork in Punjab. Identified the Elaboration/Embellishment mixing strategy — qualitative themes providing explanatory depth for quantitative patterns of female mobility, and showing how cultural norms, socioeconomic status, and age confounded the survey-level relationships in ways the numbers alone could not reveal.

Quantitative: STATA bivariate analysis of national fertility survey; accompanied vs. unaccompanied mobility stratified by demographics
Qualitative: 5-month ethnographic fieldwork across 4 data collection phases; principal researcher embedded in community
Mixing strategy: Elaboration/Embellishment — qualitative contextualizes and explains quantitative patterns rather than testing separate hypotheses
Reflection
This study made concrete something that's easy to state abstractly: quantitative data shows you that something is happening; qualitative data shows you why that's actually the case. The survey found that age and socioeconomic status predicted mobility. The ethnography explained that this was because older women had accumulated social capital that entitled them to move independently — a mechanism no survey question could have captured directly.
